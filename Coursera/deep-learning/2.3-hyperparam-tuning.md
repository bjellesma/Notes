# Hyperparameter Tuning

Often the most important hyperparameter to try out first is the **learning rate**, often denoted as $\alpha$. 

When tuning hyperparameters on a dataset, it's always a good idea to try out hyperparameters at random on the logarithmic scale. This is because you're allocating equal exploration budget to each order of magnitude. In python, that would be something like

```python
lr = 10 ** np.random.uniform(-4, 0)  # samples from 10^-4 to 10^0
```

# Batch Normalization

The idea of **batch normalization** is to stabalize your inputs by you subtract the mean and divide by the standard deviation.

$$
\mu = \frac{1}{m} \sum_i z^{(i)}
$$

$$
\sigma^2 = \frac{1}{m} \sum_i (z^{(i)} - \mu)^2
$$

$$
z^{(i)}_{\text{norm}} = \frac{z^{(i)} - \mu}{\sqrt{\sigma^2 + \varepsilon}}
$$

$$
\tilde{z}^{(i)} = \gamma z^{(i)}_{\text{norm}} + \beta
$$

Notice that gamma sets the variance and Beta sets the mean/shift for $\tilde{z}^{(i)}$

z tilde is the new normed input. Epsilon is a negligable constant used for numerical stability (so that the denominator doesn't equal zero)

In neural networks, computing batch normalization happens between the value that arrives at the neuron z, and the activation function, a

During training, batch norm computes μ and σ² from the current mini-batch. But at test time you're often evaluating one example at a time — you can't compute a meaningful mean or variance from a single example. 

During training, you keep a running exponentially weighted average of μ and σ² across all the mini-batches you've seen. Something like:
μ_running = 0.9 * μ_running + 0.1 * μ_batch
σ²_running = 0.9 * σ²_running + 0.1 * σ²_batch
Then at test time you use those running estimates instead of computing from the batch. This gives you a stable, dataset-wide estimate of the distribution rather than relying on whatever single example you're evaluating. This is because it weights recent batches more heavily, which reflects the network's actual learned distribution better than a simple average.

# Programming Frameworks

We'll look at TensorFlow. 

The following is a simple example of finding w**2 - 10*w + 25

```python
import numpy as np
import tensorflow as tf

w = tf.Variable(0, dtype=tf.float32)
optimizer = tf.keras.optimizers.Adam(0.1)

def train_step():
    with tf.GradientTape() as tape:
        cost = w ** 2 - 10 * w + 25
    trainable_variables = [w]
    grads = tape.gradient(cost, trainable_variables)
    optimizer.apply_gradients(zip(grads, trainable_variables))

print(w)  # <tf.Variable 'Variable:0' shape=() dtype=float32, numpy=0.0>

train_step()
print(w)  # <tf.Variable 'Variable:0' shape=() dtype=float32, numpy=0.09999997>

for i in range(1000):
    train_step()
print(w)  # <tf.Variable 'Variable:0' shape=() dtype=float32, numpy=5.000001>
```

Notice that tensorflow automatically figures out backprop for you.
